Common questions about where, when, and how to swim safely.

🗺️ Where to Swim

Finding a beach — and knowing whether it's open before you go.

How do I know if a beach is open for swimming?

Check the dashboard: it shows the official state status for the beach you select, based on the Massachusetts Department of Public Health's water quality testing. A beach posted as "Closed" has exceeded bacteria limits or has another active hazard (like a harmful algae bloom or a sewage release), and the dashboard shows the stated closure reason when the state provides one.

What resources can I use to gauge water safety?

  • Massachusetts DPH Beach Water Quality Dashboard — the official source for test results and beach postings statewide (and the source of this site's data).
  • MWRA: Combined Sewer Overflows — explains CSOs and tracks discharge notifications for the Boston-area sewer system.
  • Mystic River Watershed Association — local water quality monitoring and advocacy for the Mystic River watershed.
  • EPA BEACH Act resources — general guidance on beach water quality and health.

Is a posted "Open" status a guarantee the water is safe?

No. Sampling is typically weekly during the season, so conditions can change between tests — especially after rain. Treat the posted status as one input alongside recent weather, water clarity, and your own judgment.

🛟 Swimming Safely

How weather and water conditions affect whether it's a good day to swim.

Should I swim after it rains?

Rule of thumb: wait 24–72 hours after heavy rain before swimming.

Heavy rain washes bacteria and pollutants from streets, storm drains, and — in some communities — overflowing sewers directly into lakes and rivers. Bacteria levels are usually highest right after a storm and typically return to normal within a few days. Because official testing is weekly, a beach may still be posted "Open" from a sample taken before the rain, so the waiting period is your best protection after a storm.

What is a CSO?

A CSO (Combined Sewer Overflow) happens in older sewer systems where sewage and stormwater share the same pipes. In dry weather everything flows to a treatment plant, but during heavy rain the combined volume can exceed the system's capacity. To prevent sewage from backing up into streets and homes, the excess — a mix of stormwater and untreated or partially treated sewage — discharges directly into rivers, lakes, and harbors. Swimming should be avoided near a recent CSO discharge. Learn more at the MWRA's CSO page.

When a CSO event is reported near the Mystic Lakes, the dashboard shows a warning card with the incident details.

What do the bacteria test results mean?

Massachusetts tests freshwater beaches for indicator bacteria — E. coli and Enterococci — whose presence suggests possible sewage or animal-waste contamination. When a single sample or the running geometric mean of recent samples exceeds the state threshold, the beach is posted against swimming until a follow-up sample passes.

What else should I watch for?

  • Unusually murky water, surface scum, or a green paint-like sheen (possible harmful algae bloom).
  • Strong odors, visible debris, or discolored water after storms.
  • Posted advisories at the beach itself — on-site signage can be more current than online status.

🤝 Swim Together

Water quality is only part of swimming safety — company and visibility matter too.

Why shouldn't I swim alone?

Most drownings happen when no one is nearby to help. Swim with a buddy, and prefer beaches and hours with lifeguards on duty. Even strong swimmers can be surprised by cold water, cramps, or fatigue in open water.

How can I make open-water swimming safer?

  • Swim parallel to shore rather than straight out, and know your exit points.
  • Use a brightly colored swim buoy or cap so boats and companions can see you.
  • Enter cold water gradually — sudden immersion can cause gasping and panic.
  • Keep close, active watch on children; designate an adult "water watcher."
